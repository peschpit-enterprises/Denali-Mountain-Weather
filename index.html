MT. MCKINLEY - DENALI WEATHER

The book Minus 148 Degrees, written by American climber Art Davidson, regards his first winter ascent of Denali in 1967. 90 mph winds blowing air temperatures around -67F translates to a wind chill of around -148F. That number hasn’t been rivaled lately. Since the end of the previous century, the coldest observed wind chill on Denali was -118F (Nov. 2003), made by much slower, 18 mph winds but a colder air temperature: -74F.

When it gets that cold, in the late fall and winter, most climbers don’t have an imminent ascent on their minds. The wintertime Aleutian Low intermittently brings heavy snow and strong winds to Denali. April to June is the best general window for climbing, accommodated by the perennial occurrence of the Low shifting away from the region for the season. Even in the ideal timeframe, an absence of dangerous storms is far from guaranteed.

Great times to climb are characteristized by a blocking ridge of high pressure - an “Omega Block” - which tends to push potential inclement weather out of the region. Making sure the jet stream won’t be overhead helps to mitigate the threat of high winds. Meteorologists also use other general guidelines for favorable weather patterns, as well as observed and forecast data, to help prepare climbers for notable-to-dangerous rapid forecast changes.

CUSTOM FORECASTS

After waiting, stranded at basecamp, watching the wind whoop the tents for three days straight, climbers want to know when the journeys they’ve been looking forward to will become practical. Free forecasts are available but they aren’t well tailored for the exact location and haven’t been accurate. It’s already weeks deep into the trip and vacation time is starting to run out. Requesting professional meteorological insight is likely a small price to pay to maximize the chances of reaching the summit.

Forecasting for mountains since the 1980s, we cover all of the world’s major climbing areas and are especially experienced with the weather of the Alaska Range: mountains such as Denali, Mount Hunter, Moose’s Tooth and Mount Huntington.

An important part of modern forecasting is analyzing the major numerical weather prediction (NWP) models. The first NWP, created in the 1920s, was a very crude, loosely accurate, handwritten attempt that took two weeks to calculate and draw. About thirty years later came the first crude computer-generated forecast. Currently, there are over twenty-four NWP models, each specializing in either short, medium or long-term predictions. Interpreting which to use and what data is most trustworthy is developed by years of professional-level practice.



When analyzing the weather, we first monitor which models are performing most accurately. Spaghetti plots graphically compare several NWP models and average them out. If the variation is small, the forecast certainty is reasonably high. Next considered are ensemble forecasts, which make minor changes to initial conditions to calculate how that changes the forecast. Also considered are local variables, such as snowpack, shady mountain faces, vegetation cover, snowmelt, etc., which, if logically applied to the forecast, can improve upon model output. A typical, simple forecast can be obtained for $75.00.

WEATHER FORECASTS

Copious forecast data is available for free, while many more professional tools require paid subscriptions. For Alaska, the NWS Fairbanks office provides “Mount McKinley Recreational Forecasts,” radio forecasts and can be called at 800-472-0391. The ECMWF provides great forecast models for Denali. Another solid source is the US models. Among all meteorological variables, some models specialize in snow forecasting, others focus on summit conditions based on grid coordinates, etc.

Understanding the limitations of the usefulness of specific meteorological tools, however, is not common knowledge: it comes from years of forecasting with them and verifying observed conditions. Remote mountainous areas do have publicly-available raw data and forecasts but they aren’t well represented by mountain-level meteorological observations. The tools often require a professional analysis before interpreted as evidence of accurate meteorological conclusions. The extra thought may be game-changing if models are confusing key details and a big wet, windy or electrical storm is coming.
